Ada Lovelace, 1815-1852

The first ever programmer, Ada Lovelace, was born in London on December 10th 1815. Her parents
seperated when she was young and she never personally knew her father, in her younger years she was tutored
privately. Eventually she began to self-teach with the help of a reknown mathematician-logician, Augustus De
Morgan, her studies and curiosity would lead her to becoming interested in Babbage's machines and to becoming
the first ever programmer.

It was in 1833 when Ada Lovelace stumbled upon Babbage's machine, she was fascinated by it and ended up
translating a French Article by Luigi Menabrea explaining the Analytical Engine. With this translation Ada added her
own notes to the article which includes a sequence of operations for solving certain mathematical problems, these
operations were the first published "lines of code" that would be used to help advance the Analytical Machine. Another
thing Ada included in the article is the prediction that eventually we won't be programming with numbers and with
words that have a numerical value to the machine. Biographers debate the importance of Ada's contribution but one
thing is for certain, that she is the first ever programmer who paved the way for modern programming.

Today, modern programming would not be what it is without Ada Lovelace's contribution to Babbage's Analytical
Machine. The syntax or even the programming languages themselves could be entirely different if it weren't for her
developing the first instance of the structure of programming. Even if what she did had a minor impact on how we program
today, her contribution is still noteworthy in discussing the history of how computers and modern technology came to
fruition.
